# Manual Lever Dialling for Beginners

Manual lever machines (Cafelat Robot, Flair, La Pavoni) are unique because they decouple the relationship between grind size and flow rate. On a pump machine, the pump dictates the flow. On a lever, *you* dictate the flow by how much force you apply against the puck's resistance.

In this guide we will look at the variables that determine what ends up in your cup.

---

## 1. Dose, Prep, and Geometry
On a manual machine, you can imagine your hands as the sensor. Poor puck prep leads to noise in your tactile feedback.

* Lock in your Dose: Find the sweet spot for your basket, as explained in the [Dialing In Basics](./beginner.html).
* WDT/shaking is Mandatory: Use a needle tool or a shaker to ensure the density of the puck is uniform. On a lever, any weak spot in the puck will be exploited the moment you apply force. This will just make the experience miserable.

---

## 2. Dialing by Feel: Grind and Flow
Technically, the Feel is just Puck Resistance. 

* Too Fine: The puck is too dense. Even at high pressure, the flow is stalled. This increases contact time, leading to over-extracted bitterness.
* Too Coarse: The puck offers no resistance. Water gushes through, leading to low contact time and under-extracted acidity.
* Good: The grind size window for good shots is quite large on manual machines, so once you're out of the 2 zones mentioned above it will be quite easy to dial in the grind size for your preferences. 

---

## 3. The Workflow
You can think of your extraction in three phases:

### Pre-Infusion
Gently lower the lever (1–2 bars) to fill the headspace.
* Wait for the bottom of the basket to be fully beaded. This ensures the entire puck is participating when you begin the next phase.

### Ramp-up
Smoothly ramp up to your peak pressure (typically 6–8 bars for Traditional espresso, 3-6 for Turbo).
* Flow and Pressure vs. Flavor: Check out [Espresso Profiling](./profiling.html)

### Taper
As the shot progresses, the puck loses mass. If you keep the pressure constant, the flow will accelerate.
* Gradually ease off the lever to maintain a steady flow or a slight decline. This prevents the extraction of harsh, late-stage bitter compounds.

---

## 4. Saving Shots:
Manual machines allow you to rescue a bad shot by changing the extraction variables mid-pull.

### Gusher 
**Problem:** Resistance is too low, flow is too high.

**The Save:** 
* Drop the pressure: Stop pushing hard on the lever, you're now transitioning into a **turbo shot**. Drop down to the highest pressure that doesn't make the shot spray out (e.g. 7 -> 3 bars).
* Extend the ratio: Pull the shot longer (e.g. 1:3). The extra water will help extract more sweetness to balance the underextracted sourness due to the coarse grind.

### Choke
**Problem:** Resistance is too high, water can't permeate the puck.

**The Save:**
* Bloom it: Extend the pre-infusion, you're now doing a **blooming shot**. Let the puck sit at 1 bar and saturate for however long necessary for the bottom to completely bead up and start dripping. This can sometimes take 30-60 seconds.  
* Gentle Pressure: Ramp up gently to the highest pressure that doesn't make the shot spray out. 
* Reduce the ratio: These shots get bitter faster because of the high amount of contact time and fines, so you will want to pull the shot shorter (e.g. 1:1.5). 

> ### Channeling tip
> If you feel the lever "give way" (a channel has formed), ease off the pressure instantly. Dropping to 2–3 bars allows the puck to relax and sometimes "reseal" the hole, saving the cup from becoming an undrinkable astringent mess.
